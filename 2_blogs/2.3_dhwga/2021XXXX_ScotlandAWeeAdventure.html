I think this was my first ever distillery tour, so it was all really quite interesting and informative. Here's what I learned about Glengoyne:

  • Glengoyne is a Highland Single Malt Whiskey
    • Highland - the region it's distilled in (the other regions are Islay, Speyside, Lowland, Islands, and Campbeltown)
    • Single Malt - all the whiskey comes from a single malt. Compare this to a Blended which marries (blends) multiple whiskeys to achieve a certain taste profile
    • Whiskey - yes, there are specifics here. It must be aged for at least 3 years and be above 40% alcohol to be able to call itself a Scottish Whiskey
  • Glengoyne is 100% renewable energy powered - hell yeah!
  • The Daff/Draff (the result after draining the liquid wort) is no longer used for animal feed, but rather as fuel for biogas energy generation - hell yeah!
  • The only step of the distillation process that isn't done onsite at Glengoyne is the malting of the barley
  • Glengoyne is part of a small group of distilleries that still use wooden washbacks
    • Washbacks are where the liquid wort ends up after passing through a cooler, where it combined with yeast to start the alcohol-making process
    • The wood for wooden washbacks comes for the Great State of Oregon!!! Oregon Pine is the desired species of wood used for these, because the branches are all at the top of the tree. As Washbacks are usually a good 14-20' tall, and it holds nothing but a giant volume of water, water-tightness is key. With all the branches at the top of the tree, that means the main trunk of an Oregon Pine is knot-less, which helps keep everything nice and well sealed up. Go Oregon!!!
    • A full-size wooden washback can cost around $60,000. Dayum.

  • Another unique bit about Glengoyne - they're considered a slow distiller
    • This means that they perform the second of two distillations at a much slower rate
    • Rather than processing the spirit through a single still at a given rate (i.e. 1 still at 10L/min), they use two smaller stills for the second distillation at a slower rate (i.e. 2 stills at 5L/min each)
